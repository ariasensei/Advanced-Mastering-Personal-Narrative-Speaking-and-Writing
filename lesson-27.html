Part 2: Writing About Arts and Literature

Your Writing Guides

When writing about arts and literature, your writing should be:

  1. Analytical: Break down the work into its components and explain their function.
  2. Interpretive: Offer your understanding of the work's meaning.
  3. Evidentiary: Support your claims with specific details or quotes from the work.
  4. Structured: Follow a clear essay format.

Writing Structure: The Literary Analysis of a Short Story or Poem

This structure will help you write a clear and insightful analysis:

  • Title: Reflective of your analysis (e.g., "The Symbolism of Light in 'The Lighthouse'").
  • Introduction (1 paragraph):
  • Hook: Start with a general statement about the power of literature/art.
  • Background: Introduce the specific work (title, author/artist, genre).
  • Thesis Statement: State your main argument or interpretation about the work's meaning or a key element you will analyze.
  • Example Thesis: "In [Author's Name]'s short story '[Story Title]', the recurring imagery of darkness and light symbolizes the protagonist's internal struggle between despair and hope, ultimately conveying a compelling message about resilience."
  • Body Paragraph 1: Analyzing a Key Element/Theme (1 paragraph):
  • Topic Sentence: Introduce the first element or theme you will analyze.
  • Elaboration: Provide specific examples or quotes from the text/artwork. Explain how this element contributes to the overall meaning or mood.
  • Example: "The pervasive mood of isolation is established through the setting of the remote island. The author portrays the lighthouse as a symbol of both guidance and entrapment, evoking a sense of loneliness."
  • Body Paragraph 2: Further Analysis/Impact (1 paragraph):
  • Topic Sentence: Introduce a second element or further develop your analysis of the first.
  • Elaboration: Continue to provide evidence and explain its significance. Discuss how the element illustrates a theme or resonates with the reader.
  • Example: "Furthermore, the protagonist's internal conflict is highlighted through the recurring metaphor of a stormy sea, which captures his emotional turmoil. This imagery makes his journey particularly thought-provoking."
  • Conclusion (1 paragraph):
  • Summarize: Briefly restate your main analytical points.
  • Reiterate Thesis: Rephrase your thesis statement.
  • Final Thought: Offer a broader reflection on the work's enduring message or its artistic merit.

Writing Practice: Your Literary Analysis

  1. Outline Your Analysis: Choose a short story, poem, or even a specific scene from a movie or a painting that you want to analyze. Focus on one or two key elements (e.g., theme, symbolism, character development, mood). Create a quick outline for a short analysis (2-3 paragraphs) following the structure above.
  2. Draft Your Analysis: Start writing your literary analysis. Focus on:
  • Clearly stating your interpretation.
  • Supporting your claims with specific evidence.
  • Using analytical and evaluative vocabulary.
  • Organizing your ideas logically.
