Part 1: Speaking About Global Citizenship

Your Speaking Guides

When discussing global citizenship, these guides will help you:

  1. Broaden Your Perspective: Think beyond your local community to a global scale.
  2. Identify Interconnections: Understand how local actions can have global consequences.
  3. Propose Solutions: Focus on how individuals and groups can contribute to global well-being.
  4. Use Formal and Persuasive Language: Convey the importance of global issues and actions.
  5. Speak with Conviction and Empathy: Show genuine concern for global challenges.

Vocabulary Builder: Words for Global Citizenship

Let's build your word bank! Remember to write these down in your notebook, and add any new ones you learn!

Global Concepts:

  • Global citizen: An individual who identifies with the global community and works to build a more just and sustainable world.
  • Interconnectedness: The state of being connected with each other.
  • Human rights: Rights inherent to all human beings, regardless of race, sex, nationality, ethnicity, language, religion, or any other status.
  • Social justice: Justice in terms of the distribution of wealth, opportunities, and privileges within a society.
  • Equality: The state of being equal, especially in status, rights, or opportunities.
  • Equity: The quality of being fair and impartial. (Often refers to fairness in distribution, not just sameness).
  • Sustainable development: Development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.
  • Poverty: The state of being extremely poor.
  • Inequality: Difference in size, degree, circumstances, etc.; lack of equality.
  • Climate crisis: A severe and urgent problem caused by climate change.
  • Humanitarian aid: Material or logistical assistance provided for humanitarian purposes, typically in response to humanitarian crises.
  • Advocacy: Public support for or recommendation of a particular cause or policy.
  • Collaboration: The action of working with someone to produce or create something.

Actions & Responsibilities:

  • Responsibility: The state or fact of having a duty to deal with something or of having control over someone.
  • Accountability: The fact or condition of being accountable; responsibility.
  • Empathy: The ability to understand and share the feelings of another.
  • Tolerance: The ability or willingness to tolerate the existence of opinions or behavior that one dislikes or disagrees with.
  • Engagement: The action of engaging or being engaged. (Often refers to civic or global engagement).
  • Contribute to: To help to cause or bring about.
  • Participate in: To take part in.
  • Promote: To support or actively encourage (a cause, venture, etc.).
  • Address: To deal with or discuss.
  • Mitigate: To make less severe, serious, or painful.

Grammar Focus: Expressing Responsibility and Persuasion

These structures will help you discuss global responsibilities and persuade others:

Expressing Obligation/Necessity:

  • It is our responsibility to [action]. (e.g., "It is our responsibility to address global poverty.")
  • We must [action] in order to [goal].
  • It is imperative that we [action].
  • Individuals/Governments ought to [action].
  • There is a moral obligation to [action].

Using "If...Then" for Global Scenarios:

  • If we fail to [action], then [negative consequence] will occur. (e.g., "If we fail to mitigate the climate crisis, then future generations will face severe environmental challenges.")
  • If [positive action] is implemented, then [positive outcome] can be achieved.

Persuasive Connectors:

  • Therefore, ...
  • Consequently, ...
  • As a result, ...
  • It is evident that...
  • Furthermore, ...
  • Moreover, ...

Speaking Practice: Discussing Global Responsibilities

  1. Choose a Global Challenge: Select a global challenge that you feel strongly about (e.g., global poverty, climate crisis, human rights violations, access to education).
  2. Explain the Challenge: Briefly describe the challenge and its impact on people globally.
  3. Discuss Responsibilities: Whose responsibility is it to address this challenge (individuals, governments, international organizations)?
  4. Propose Actions: What specific actions can be taken to mitigate or solve this problem? How can we promote sustainable development?
  5. Discuss: Practice discussing your chosen global challenge and responsibilities. Set a timer for 2-3 minutes for this practice. Try to use:
  • At least 3-4 new vocabulary words from the list.
  • At least 1-2 sentences expressing obligation or necessity.
  • Phrases for proposing solutions and showing cause/effect.
