Part 1: Speaking About Media

Your Speaking Guides

When discussing media, these guides will help you:

  1. Be Critical: Don't just accept information; think about its source and purpose.
  2. Use Evidence: Support your opinions with examples from media you've seen.
  3. Acknowledge Different Views: Understand that others might have different interpretations.
  4. Use Analytical Language: Employ words that show you're thinking deeply about the topic.
  5. Speak with Clarity: Express complex ideas in an understandable way.

Vocabulary Builder: Words for Media Analysis

Let's build your word bank! Remember to write these down in your notebook, and add any new ones you learn!

Media Types & Concepts:

  • News report: An account of recent events.
  • Social media: Online platforms for communication and sharing.
  • Advertisement: A public notice promoting a product, service, or event.
  • Propaganda: Information, especially of a biased or misleading nature, used to promote a political cause or point of view.
  • Bias: Prejudice in favor of or against one thing, person, or group compared with another, usually in a way considered to be unfair.
  • Credibility: The quality of being believable or trustworthy.
  • Source: The origin of information.
  • Target audience: The specific group of people an advertisement or message is aimed at.
  • Influence: The capacity to have an effect on the character, development, or behavior of someone or something.
  • Manipulation: The action of influencing or controlling someone or something to your own advantage, often unfairly.
  • Disinformation: False information, especially that which is intended to deceive.

Analytical Verbs & Phrases:

  • Analyze: To examine in detail.
  • Evaluate: To form an idea of the amount, number, or value of.
  • Interpret: To explain the meaning of.
  • Critique: To evaluate (a theory or work) in a detailed and analytical way.
  • Convey: To communicate (a message or information).
  • Portray: To depict (someone or something) in a particular way.

Grammar Focus: Expressing Nuance and Opinion

These structures will help you express more complex thoughts and opinions:

Expressing Degrees of Certainty:

  • It is likely that...
  • It is possible that...
  • It is evident that...
  • There is a strong possibility that...
  • It seems to suggest that...

Using Conditional Sentences (Type 1 & 2):

  • If [condition], then [likely result]. (e.g., "If news sources are biased, then the public may receive incomplete information.")
  • If [hypothetical condition], [hypothetical result]. (e.g., "If social media didn't exist, how would people get their news?")

Incorporating Concessions:

  • Although [counter-argument], [main point]. (e.g., "Although advertisements can be misleading, they are often a necessary part of commerce.")
  • While [point 1], [point 2].
  • Despite [noun/gerund phrase], [main point]. (e.g., "Despite the widespread use of social media, traditional news outlets still hold significant credibility.")

Speaking Practice: Discussing Media Influence

  1. Choose a Media Example: Think of a recent news report, social media post, or advertisement you've seen.
  2. Analyze It:
  • What is the main message being conveyed?
  • Who do you think is the target audience?
  • Do you detect any bias or attempt at manipulation? Explain why.
  • What impact do you think this media has on people?
  1. Discuss: Practice discussing your chosen media example and your analysis. Set a timer for 2-3 minutes for this practice. Try to use:
  • At least 3-4 new vocabulary words from the list.
  • At least 1-2 complex sentences using the structures above.
  • Phrases to express your opinion and analytical insights.
