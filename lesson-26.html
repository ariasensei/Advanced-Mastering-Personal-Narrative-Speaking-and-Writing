Part 1: Speaking About Arts and Literature

Your Speaking Guides

When discussing arts and literature, these guides will help you:

  1. Express Personal Response: Share how a work makes you feel or what it means to you.
  2. Analyze Elements: Identify and discuss specific components of the artwork or text (e.g., themes, characters, colors, sounds).
  3. Support Interpretations: Explain why you have a particular interpretation, referencing details from the work.
  4. Use Evaluative Language: Employ words that describe quality, impact, or artistic merit.
  5. Speak with Enthusiasm: Convey your appreciation for the creative work.

Vocabulary Builder: Words for Arts and Literature

Let's build your word bank! Remember to write these down in your notebook, and add any new ones you learn!

General Art & Literary Concepts:

  • Theme: The main subject or idea in a piece of writing, art, or music.
  • Symbolism: The use of symbols to represent ideas or qualities.
  • Imagery: Visually descriptive or figurative language, especially in a literary work.
  • Metaphor: A figure of speech in which a word or phrase is applied to an object or action to which it is not literally applicable.
  • Simile: A figure of speech involving the comparison of one thing with another thing of a different kind, used to make a description more emphatic or vivid (e.g., as brave as a lion, crazy like a fox).
  • Mood: The atmosphere or a pervading tone of something, especially a work of art.
  • Tone: The general character or attitude of a place, piece of writing, situation, etc.
  • Genre: A category of artistic composition, as in music or literature, characterized by similarities in form, style, or subject matter.
  • Narrative: A spoken or written account of connected events; a story.
  • Protagonist: The leading character or one of the major characters in a drama, movie, novel, or other fictional text.
  • Antagonist: A person who actively opposes or is hostile to someone or something; an adversary.
  • Setting: The place or type of surroundings where something is positioned or where an event takes place.
  • Plot: The main events of a play, novel, movie, or similar work, devised and presented by the writer as an interrelated sequence.

Evaluative & Analytical Terms:

  • Evokes: Bring or recall to the conscious mind.
  • Portrays: Depict (someone or something) in a work of art or literature.
  • Illustrates: Explain or make clear by using examples, charts, etc.
  • Captures: Succeed in representing or expressing something intangible.
  • Resonates with: Evokes images, memories, or emotions.
  • Masterpiece: A work of outstanding artistry, skill, or workmanship.
  • Compelling: Evoking interest, attention, or admiration in a powerfully irresistible way.
  • Thought-provoking: Causing one to think seriously about something.
  • Nuance: A subtle difference in or shade of meaning, expression, or sound.
  • Interpretation: The action of explaining the meaning of something.

Grammar Focus: Expressing Analysis and Interpretation

These structures will help you articulate your understanding of creative works:

Attributing Meaning/Effect:

  • The artist/author uses [element] to convey [message/theme]. (e.g., "The author uses vivid imagery to convey the protagonist's despair.")
  • [Element] symbolizes [idea].
  • This piece evokes a sense of [emotion].
  • The [mood/tone] of the work is established through [technique].

Supporting Interpretations:

  • For instance, when the character [action], it illustrates [point].
  • An example of this is evident in [specific detail].
  • This is highlighted by the phrase/color/sound [quote/description].

Expressing Personal Response:

  • I find this piece [adjective] because...
  • What resonates with me most is...
  • It makes me feel [emotion] when I consider...
  • My interpretation is that...

Speaking Practice: Discussing a Work of Art or Literature

  1. Choose a Work: Select a favorite painting, song, movie scene, short story, or poem.
  2. Describe It: Briefly describe the work (what it is, who created it).
  3. Analyze Elements: What are some key elements (e.g., theme, imagery, mood, characters)?
  4. Share Your Interpretation: What do you think the artist/author is trying to convey? What does it evoke in you?
  5. Discuss: Practice discussing your chosen work. Set a timer for 2-3 minutes for this practice. Try to use:
  • At least 3-4 new vocabulary words from the list.
  • At least 1-2 sentences expressing analysis or interpretation.
  • Phrases to support your ideas with examples.
