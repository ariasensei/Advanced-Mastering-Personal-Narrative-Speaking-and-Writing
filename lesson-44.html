Part 1: Speaking About Academic Work

Your Speaking Guides

When discussing academic work, these guides will help you:

  1. Be Objective: Present information fairly, based on the text or research, not just personal opinion.
  2. Summarize Accurately: Condense complex information into its main points.
  3. Analyze Critically: Identify strengths, weaknesses, and implications of research or arguments.
  4. Use Formal Language: Employ academic vocabulary and structures.
  5. Speak with Precision and Clarity: Articulate complex ideas in an understandable way.

Vocabulary Builder: Words for Academic Skills

Let's build your word bank! Remember to write these down in your notebook, and add any new ones you learn!

Research & Analysis:

  • Research: The systematic investigation into and study of materials and sources in order to establish facts and reach new conclusions.
  • Hypothesis: A supposition or proposed explanation made on the basis of limited evidence as a starting point for further investigation.
  • Methodology: A system of methods used in a particular area of study or activity.
  • Data: Facts and statistics collected together for reference or analysis.
  • Evidence: The available body of facts or information indicating whether a belief or proposition is true or valid.
  • Findings: The results of an investigation.
  • Conclusion: The end or finish of an event or process. (Also, a judgment or decision reached by reasoning).
  • Implication: The conclusion that can be drawn from something although it is not explicitly stated.
  • Validity: The quality of being logically or factually sound; soundness or cogency.
  • Reliability: The quality of being trustworthy or of performing consistently well.
  • Bias: Prejudice in favor of or against one thing, person, or group compared with another, usually in a way considered to be unfair.

Summarizing & Critiquing:

  • Summarize: To give a brief statement of the main points of something.
  • Paraphrase: To express the meaning of (something written or spoken) using different words, especially to achieve greater clarity.
  • Synthesize: To combine (a number of things) into a coherent whole.
  • Critique: A detailed analysis and assessment of something, especially a literary, philosophical, or political theory.
  • Evaluate: To form an idea of the amount, number, or value of.
  • Assess: To evaluate or estimate the nature, ability, or quality of.
  • Strengths: The qualities or features of a person or thing that make them effective or successful.
  • Weaknesses: The state or condition of being weak. (Often refers to flaws or deficiencies).
  • Argument: A reason or set of reasons given with the aim of persuading others that an action or idea is right or wrong.
  • Claim: An assertion of the truth of something, typically one that is disputed or in doubt.
  • Counter-argument: An argument or set of reasons put forward to oppose an idea or theory developed in another argument.

Grammar Focus: Presenting Information and Evaluating Arguments

These structures will help you effectively present and critique academic content:

Presenting Research Findings:

  • The study found that... / The research indicates that...
  • According to [author/source], [information].
  • The data suggests that...
  • This aligns with previous research which demonstrated...
  • The methodology employed involved...

Summarizing & Paraphrasing:

  • The author argues that...
  • In essence, the main point is...
  • To summarize, ...
  • [Author] contends that [idea]. (More formal than "argues")
  • The article primarily focuses on...

Critiquing & Evaluating:

  • A key strength of this argument is its reliance on [evidence].
  • However, a significant weakness lies in its [flaw].
  • The validity of the findings could be questioned due to [reason].
  • While the author makes a compelling claim, the evidence provided is [adjective, e.g., limited/strong].
  • The implications of this research are far-reaching, suggesting that...

Speaking Practice: Presenting Research Findings

  1. Choose an Academic Topic: Think about a topic you've researched for a school project, or a scientific concept you understand well (e.g., photosynthesis, a historical event, a literary theme).
  2. Summarize Key Information: What are the main findings or conclusions?
  3. Explain Methodology (if applicable): Briefly describe how the information was gathered or studied (e.g., "This was based on a survey," "I researched historical documents").
  4. Discuss Implications/Significance: Why is this information important? What are its implications?
  5. Discuss: Practice presenting your chosen academic topic. Set a timer for 2-3 minutes for this practice. Try to use:
  • At least 3-4 new vocabulary words from the list.
  • At least 1-2 sentences for presenting findings or discussing implications.
  • Phrases for summarizing.
