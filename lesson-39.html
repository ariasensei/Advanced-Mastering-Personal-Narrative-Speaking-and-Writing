Part 2: Writing About Ethics

Your Writing Guides

When writing about ethics and morality, your writing should be:

  1. Argumentative: Present a clear thesis and support it with logical reasoning.
  2. Analytical: Break down the ethical issue into its core components.
  3. Principled: Base your arguments on clear moral principles or values.
  4. Structured: Follow an essay format with clear arguments.

Writing Structure: The Argumentative Essay on a Moral Issue

This structure will help you write a clear and persuasive argumentative essay:

  • Title: Thought-provoking and clear (e.g., "The Moral Obligation of Honesty in Friendship").
  • Introduction (1 paragraph):
  • Hook: Start with a general statement about the importance of ethics or the complexity of moral choices.
  • Background: Introduce the specific moral issue or dilemma you will discuss.
  • Thesis Statement: Clearly state your main argument – what is the most ethical or moral stance on this issue, and why?
  • Example Thesis: "While loyalty to friends is a cherished value, upholding the principle of honesty is a greater moral obligation, as it fosters integrity and long-term trust in any relationship."
  • Body Paragraph 1: Presenting the Ethical Conflict (1 paragraph):
  • Topic Sentence: Elaborate on the moral issue, explaining the conflicting principles or values at play.
  • Elaboration: Describe the dilemma and the different sides or considerations involved. Use relevant vocabulary.
  • Example: "The dilemma often arises when a friend's actions conflict with one's own sense of right and wrong. The core conflict here is between the value of loyalty and the principle of truthfulness."
  • Body Paragraph 2: Justifying Your Moral Stance (1 paragraph):
  • Topic Sentence: Present your main argument for the most ethical or moral approach.
  • Elaboration: Provide logical reasoning, ethical principles, and potential positive consequences to support your stance. Discuss why this choice is justifiable.
  • Example: "I would argue that choosing honesty is the most ethical decision. This stance is justified by the long-term consequences of deceit, which can erode trust and damage relationships, ultimately undermining the very integrity of friendship."
  • Conclusion (1 paragraph):
  • Summarize: Briefly restate the moral issue and your justified stance.
  • Reiterate Thesis: Rephrase your thesis statement.
  • Final Thought: Offer a broader reflection on the importance of ethical reasoning in daily life or the enduring nature of moral principles.

Writing Practice: Your Moral Issue Essay

  1. Outline Your Essay: Choose a specific moral issue or ethical dilemma that you can argue for a particular stance (e.g., the ethics of animal testing, the morality of certain social media behaviors, academic honesty, environmental responsibility). Create a quick outline for a short argumentative essay (2-3 paragraphs) following the structure above.
  2. Draft Your Essay: Start writing your argumentative essay. Focus on:
  • Clearly stating your thesis.
  • Supporting your arguments with logical reasoning and ethical principles.
  • Using precise vocabulary related to ethics and morality.
  • Organizing your ideas persuasively.
