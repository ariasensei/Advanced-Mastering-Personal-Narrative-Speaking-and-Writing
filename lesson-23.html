Part 1: Speaking About History

Your Speaking Guides

When discussing historical events, these guides will help you:

  1. Be Factual: Refer to accurate information about the event.
  2. Explain Causes and Effects: Clearly link actions to their consequences.
  3. Analyze Significance: Discuss why the event was important and its long-term impact.
  4. Use Formal Language: Employ vocabulary appropriate for academic historical discussion.
  5. Speak with Clarity and Coherence: Present information in a logical and easy-to-follow manner.

Vocabulary Builder: Words for Historical Events and Their Impact

Let's build your word bank! Remember to write these down in your notebook, and add any new ones you learn!

Historical Concepts:

  • Era: A long and distinct period of history.
  • Epoch: A period in history or a person's life marked by notable events or particular characteristics.
  • Turning point: A time at which a decisive change in a situation occurs.
  • Legacy: Something handed down from an ancestor or from the past.
  • Consequence: A result or effect of an action or condition.
  • Catalyst: A person or thing that precipitates an event.
  • Precedent: An earlier event or action that is regarded as an example or guide to be considered in subsequent similar circumstances.
  • Movement: A group of people working together to advance their shared political, social, or artistic ideas.
  • Revolution: A forcible overthrow of a government or social order, in favor of a new system.
  • Reform: The improvement or amendment of what is wrong, corrupt, or unsatisfactory.

Analytical Verbs & Phrases:

  • Emerged: To become apparent, important, or prominent.
  • Triggered: To cause (an event or situation) to happen or exist.
  • Led to: Resulted in.
  • Contributed to: Helped to cause or bring about.
  • Shaped: Had a significant influence on the form of (something).
  • Impacted: Had a strong effect on someone or something.
  • Influenced: Had an effect on the character, development, or behavior of someone or something.
  • Resulted in: Caused something to happen.
  • Significance: The quality of being worthy of attention; importance.
  • Profound: Very great or intense.
  • Lasting effect: An effect that continues for a long time.

Grammar Focus: Explaining Historical Causality and Impact

These structures will help you explain how historical events unfold and their effects:

Expressing Cause and Effect (Advanced):

  • [Event A] served as a catalyst for [Event B]. (e.g., "The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand served as a catalyst for World War I.")
  • [Factor] played a crucial role in [outcome].
  • The repercussions of [event] included [consequence 1] and [consequence 2].
  • [Event] had a profound impact on [area].
  • This led to a series of events that culminated in [final outcome].

Using Past Perfect for Sequence:

  • By the time [event 2 happened], [event 1 had already happened]. (e.g., "By the time the treaty was signed, many lives had already been lost.")
  • After [event 1 had occurred], [event 2 took place].

Discussing Lasting Effects:

  • The legacy of [event] can still be observed in [modern example].
  • [Event] set a precedent for [future action/policy].
  • Its influence continues to shape [aspect of society].

Speaking Practice: Discussing a Historical Event

  1. Choose a Historical Event: Select a significant historical event (e.g., a major war, a scientific discovery, a social movement, a political revolution).
  2. Briefly Describe It: What was the event? When and where did it happen?
  3. Explain Causes: What factors or events led to it? What was the catalyst?
  4. Discuss Impact: What were its immediate consequences? What was its lasting effect or legacy? How did it shape society?
  5. Discuss: Practice discussing your chosen historical event. Set a timer for 2-3 minutes for this practice. Try to use:
  • At least 3-4 new vocabulary words from the list.
  • At least 1-2 sentences explaining causality or impact.
  • Phrases to discuss its significance.
