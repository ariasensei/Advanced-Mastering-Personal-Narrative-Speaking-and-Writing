Part 1: Speaking About Ethics

Your Speaking Guides

When discussing ethics and morality, these guides will help you:

  1. Define Terms: Clearly explain what you mean by "ethical" or "moral" in a given context.
  2. Analyze Dilemmas: Break down complex situations into their core moral components.
  3. Consider Different Perspectives: Acknowledge that there can be valid arguments on multiple sides.
  4. Justify Your Stance: Explain the reasoning behind your moral choices or opinions.
  5. Use Precise Language: Employ specific vocabulary to discuss abstract moral concepts.

Vocabulary Builder: Words for Ethics and Morality

Let's build your word bank! Remember to write these down in your notebook, and add any new ones you learn!

Core Concepts:

  • Ethics: Moral principles that govern a person's behavior or the conducting of an activity. (Often refers to a system of moral principles).
  • Morality: Principles concerning the distinction between right and wrong or good and bad behavior. (Often refers to personal beliefs).
  • Dilemma: A situation in which a difficult choice has to be made between two or more alternatives, especially equally undesirable ones.
  • Integrity: The quality of being honest and having strong moral principles.
  • Consequence: A result or effect of an action or condition.
  • Principle: A fundamental truth or proposition that serves as the foundation for a system of belief or behavior.
  • Value: A person's or a society's principles or standards of behavior; one's judgment of what is important in life.
  • Justice: Just behavior or treatment.
  • Fairness: Impartial and just treatment or behavior without favoritism or discrimination.
  • Responsibility: The state or fact of having a duty to deal with something or of having control over someone.
  • Accountability: The fact or condition of being accountable; responsibility.
  • Empathy: The ability to understand and share the feelings of another.
  • Compassion: Sympathetic pity and concern for the sufferings or misfortunes of others.

Actions & Qualities:

  • Ethical decision: A decision that aligns with moral principles.
  • Moral obligation: A duty that is owed to others based on moral principles.
  • Justifiable: Able to be shown to be right or reasonable; defensible.
  • Unethical: Not morally correct.
  • Conscientious: Wishing to do one's work or duty well and thoroughly.
  • Altruism: The belief in or practice of disinterested and selfless concern for the well-being of others.
  • Beneficial: Favorable or advantageous; resulting in good.
  • Harmful: Causing or likely to cause harm.
  • Uphold: To maintain or support (a custom, principle, or law).
  • Compromise: An agreement or a settlement of a dispute that is reached by each side making concessions.

Grammar Focus: Analyzing Moral Situations and Justifying Stances

These structures will help you discuss ethical dilemmas and express your moral reasoning:

Presenting a Dilemma:

  • Consider a scenario where [situation presents a conflict]. (e.g., "Consider a scenario where a friend asks you to lie for them.")
  • This situation presents an ethical dilemma because...
  • The core conflict here is between [value 1] and [value 2].

Justifying a Moral Choice:

  • I would argue that [action] is the most ethical choice because...
  • My decision is based on the principle of [principle].
  • The primary reason for this stance is that it upholds [value].
  • From a moral perspective, it is imperative to [action].

Discussing Consequences:

  • The potential consequences of [action] include [consequence 1] and [consequence 2].
  • This choice could lead to [positive/negative outcome].
  • It is important to consider the long-term implications of [action].

Speaking Practice: Discussing an Ethical Dilemma

  1. Choose an Ethical Dilemma: Think about a common ethical dilemma (e.g., honesty vs. loyalty to a friend, individual freedom vs. collective safety, environmental protection vs. economic development).
  2. Describe the Dilemma: Clearly explain the situation and the conflicting principles or values involved.
  3. Analyze Perspectives: Briefly discuss different ways someone might approach this dilemma.
  4. State Your Stance & Justify: What would you do, and why? What moral principles would guide your ethical decision?
  5. Discuss: Practice discussing your chosen ethical dilemma. Set a timer for 2-3 minutes for this practice. Try to use:
  • At least 3-4 new vocabulary words from the list.
  • At least 1-2 sentences presenting a dilemma or justifying a choice.
  • Phrases to discuss consequences and different perspectives.
