Part 2: Writing About Academic Work

Your Writing Guides

When writing about academic work, your writing should be:

  1. Objective: Present information accurately and without personal bias.
  2. Analytical: Break down arguments and evaluate their components.
  3. Concise: Summarize effectively without unnecessary detail.
  4. Formal: Use academic language and maintain a professional tone.

Writing Structure: The Summary and Critique of an Academic Article

This structure will help you write a clear and insightful summary and critique:

  • Title: Clear and descriptive (e.g., "Summary and Critique of 'The Impact of Social Media on Adolescent Well-being'").
  • Introduction (1 paragraph):
  • Hook: Start with a general statement about the importance of academic research in understanding complex issues.
  • Background: Introduce the academic article you will summarize and critique (title, author, main topic).
  • Thesis Statement: State the article's main claim or conclusion, and briefly indicate your overall evaluation (e.g., "This article effectively argues X, though its methodology has some limitations.").
  • Example Thesis: "In their article, '[Article Title]', [Author's Name] presents a compelling argument that [main claim], supported by robust data; however, a closer critique reveals certain limitations in its methodology."
  • Body Paragraph 1: Summary of Main Points (1 paragraph):
  • Topic Sentence: Introduce the article's primary focus.
  • Elaboration: Summarize the article's main arguments, findings, or conclusions in your own words. Use paraphrasing effectively. Do not include your critique here.
  • Example: "The author's central claim is that increased screen time among adolescents leads to a decline in mental health. They support this by presenting data from a large-scale survey, concluding that there is a significant correlation between social media use and reported anxiety levels."
  • Body Paragraph 2: Critique - Strengths and Weaknesses (1 paragraph):
  • Topic Sentence: Introduce your evaluation of the article's strengths and weaknesses.
  • Elaboration: Discuss specific aspects such as the validity of the evidence, the reliability of the methodology, potential bias, or the strength of the argument. Provide examples from the article.
  • Example: "A key strength of this article is its extensive data collection, which lends credibility to its findings. However, a significant weakness lies in its methodology, as it relies heavily on self-reported data, which can affect the reliability of the results."
  • Conclusion (1 paragraph):
  • Summarize: Briefly restate the article's main point and your overall evaluation.
  • Reiterate Thesis: Rephrase your thesis statement.
  • Final Thought: Offer a broader reflection on the implications of the research or suggestions for future studies.

Writing Practice: Your Summary and Critique

  1. Outline Your Essay: Choose an academic article (or a well-structured news report that presents research). Read it carefully. Create a quick outline for a short summary and critique (2-3 paragraphs) following the structure above.
  2. Draft Your Essay: Start writing your summary and critique. Focus on:
  • Accurately summarizing the original content.
  • Providing a balanced and well-reasoned critique.
  • Using formal academic vocabulary.
  • Supporting your points with references to the text.
