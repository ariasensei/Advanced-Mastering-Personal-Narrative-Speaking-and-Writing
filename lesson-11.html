Part 1: Speaking About Culture

Your Speaking Guides

When discussing cultural diversity, these guides will help you:

  1. Be Respectful: Always approach cultural topics with an open mind and respect for differences.
  2. Use Descriptive Language: Help your listener visualize cultural practices and traditions.
  3. Share Personal Experiences: Connect the topic to your own background or observations.
  4. Compare and Contrast: Be able to highlight similarities and differences between cultures.
  5. Speak with Empathy: Show understanding for diverse perspectives.

Vocabulary Builder: Words for Cultural Diversity

Let's build your word bank! Remember to write these down in your notebook, and add any new ones you learn!

Cultural Concepts:

  • Culture: The customs, arts, social institutions, and achievements of a particular nation, people, or group.
  • Tradition: A belief, custom, or way of doing something that has existed for a long time.
  • Custom: A traditional and widely accepted way of behaving or doing something specific to a particular society, place, or time.
  • Heritage: Property that is or may be inherited; an inheritance. (Often refers to cultural heritage).
  • Diversity: The practice or quality of including or involving people from a range of different social and ethnic backgrounds and of different genders, sexual orientations, etc.
  • Identity: The fact of being who or what a person or thing is. (Often refers to cultural identity).
  • Values: A person's or a society's principles or standards of behavior; one's judgment of what is important in life.
  • Norms: Rules or expectations that are socially enforced.
  • Stereotype: A widely held but fixed and oversimplified image or idea of a particular type of person or thing.
  • Ethnocentrism: Evaluation of other cultures according to preconceptions originating in the standards and customs of one's own culture.
  • Assimilation: The process of taking in and fully understanding information or ideas. (Often refers to cultural assimilation).
  • Multiculturalism: The presence of, or support for the presence of, several distinct cultural or ethnic groups within a society.

Descriptive & Comparative Language:

  • Distinctive: Characteristic of one person or thing, and so serving to distinguish it from others.
  • Unique: Being the only one of its kind; unlike anything else.
  • Vibrant: Full of energy and enthusiasm; bright and striking.
  • Rich: Having a great deal of something. (e.g., "rich cultural heritage").
  • Similar to: Having a resemblance in appearance, character, or quantity, without being identical.
  • In contrast to: Used to show that something is very different from something else.
  • Whereas: In contrast or comparison with the fact that.
  • Common ground: Shared interests, beliefs, or opinions between two or more groups of people.

Grammar Focus: Expressing Comparisons and Nuances

These structures will help you effectively compare and contrast cultural aspects:

Comparative and Superlative Adjectives/Adverbs:

  • [Culture A] is more [adjective] than [Culture B]. (e.g., "Filipino culture is often more family-oriented than some Western cultures.")
  • [Culture C] is the most [adjective] of all.
  • [Culture A] celebrates [event] as much as [Culture B] celebrates [event].

Using "While" and "Whereas" for Contrast:

  • While [aspect of Culture A], [aspect of Culture B]. (e.g., "While some cultures emphasize individualism, others prioritize community welfare.")
  • [Aspect of Culture A], whereas [aspect of Culture B].

Expressing Generalizations with Caution:

  • Generally speaking,...
  • In many cultures,...
  • It is often observed that...
  • Some believe that... (Avoid making sweeping statements about entire cultures).

Speaking Practice: Sharing Cultural Insights

  1. Choose a Cultural Aspect: Think about a specific tradition, custom, or value from your own culture or a culture you are familiar with.
  2. Describe It:
  • What is it?
  • When and how is it practiced?
  • What does it symbolize or represent?
  • What are its key characteristics (e.g., is it vibrant, distinctive?)
  1. Compare (Optional): If possible, briefly compare it to a similar or contrasting aspect in another culture.
  2. Discuss: Practice discussing your chosen cultural aspect. Set a timer for 2-3 minutes for this practice. Try to use:
  • At least 3-4 new vocabulary words from the list.
  • At least 1-2 complex sentences using the structures above.
  • Phrases to describe, compare, and express your understanding.
